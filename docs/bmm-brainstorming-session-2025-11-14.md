## Technique Sessions

### SWOT Analysis

**Strengths:**
- AI-powered recipe finding and substitution.
- Comprehensive pantry management with expiration alerts.
- Smart shopping recommendations (e.g., "soon out of milk").
- Core value proposition: reducing food waste with positive reinforcement.
- Helps users make good food.

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited development time (6 weeks).
- Reliance on free-tier tools (e.g., Spoonacular API limits).
- Implementation challenges for advanced features (barcode scanner, GPS for store sales).
- Manual data entry for pantry items is a significant friction point.
- Potential for UI/UX improvement.
- Lack of a reward system for food waste reduction.

**Opportunities:**
- Free access to all features, lowering the barrier to entry.
- AI-powered personalization for recipes, pantry tracking, dietary preferences.
- Positive encouragement for reducing food waste.
- Direct financial savings for users by reducing food waste.

**Threats:**
- Crowded market with many similar applications.
- User resistance or distrust towards AI-powered features.
- Rapid advancement of AI technology, potentially making current models obsolete.

### Assumption Reversal

**Assumption 1: We are able to make it with the time we have.**
- **Reversed:** We will NOT be able to build the proposed MVP in 6 weeks.
- **Implications:** Forces cutting extra features (GPS, barcode scanner, 3rd party login). Clarifies core MVP as AI recipe suggestions, pantry tracking, shopping list, and security functions.

**Assumption 2: Users will pick us instead of the other applications.**
- **Reversed:** Users try our app, but they DON'T switch. They go back to their old habits or a competitor's app.
- **Implications:** Primary reasons for leaving would be the friction of manual data entry and the existence of superior, better-funded competitors (especially those using paid AI). This highlights the critical importance of a seamless inventory management experience.

### "What If" Scenarios

**Scenario 1: A major tech company launches a competing free, AI-powered food waste app.**
- **Response:**
    - Differentiate on business model (ours stays free).
    - Market as an indie dev team product.
    - Investigate adding image recognition for feature parity.
    - Conduct market research to identify user preferences and competitor weaknesses.
    - Innovate with new, unique features to stand out.

**Scenario 2: Spoonacular discontinues its free tier or imposes restrictive limits.**
- **Response:**
    - Cache existing recipes from Spoonacular.
    - Actively search for alternative recipe APIs.
    - Explore and integrate free, open-source recipe databases (with safety checks).
    - Acknowledge and plan for potential project delays due to this pivot.

### Insights and Learnings

_Key realizations from the session_

- **Refined Insight 1 (Timeline Risk):** To better manage the 6-week timeline, create a "stretch goal" list of features. These are desirable but will be the first to be cut if time runs short, protecting the core MVP.
- **Refined Insight 2 (User Friction):** The "manual data entry" problem is the most critical threat. While a barcode scanner is a future goal, prioritize a "quick add" feature for common items (e.g., milk, eggs, bread) in the MVP to reduce friction immediately.
- **Refined Insight 3 (Brand Strategy):** The "indie brand" strategy needs actionable steps. This could include an "About Us" page, a blog detailing the development journey, and a clear feedback channel for users to connect directly with the developers.
- **Refined Insight 4 (API Risk):** The contingency plan for the Spoonacular API is good, but proactively research and test at least one open-source recipe database *during* the 6-week MVP development to have a warm backup ready, not just as a future plan.
- **Pre-mortem Insight 1 (Scope Overload):** A major risk of failure is spending too much time on extra features or making the project too big for the 6-week timeline, leading to an incomplete or rushed product.
- **Pre-mortem Insight 2 (Quality Compromise):** Sacrificing the quality and robustness of core features due to time pressure could result in a buggy or unreliable application, leading to user dissatisfaction.

## Action Planning

### Top 3 Priority Ideas

#### #1 Priority: Focus on Core Features (AI, Pantry, Shopping List, Security)

- Rationale: These features are the foundation of the application. Without them, the app fails to deliver its core purpose, and there's no point in building other features on a non-working foundation.
- Next steps: Add core features to the task list with a clear timeframe.
- Resources needed: The Gemini CLI (AI agent) and its various roles.
- Timeline: 1-2 weeks.

#### #2 Priority: Adhere to the Weekly Schedule

- Rationale: To ensure the project is completed within the 6-week timeframe and to avoid problems like spending too much time on one thing and not delivering on time.
- Next steps: Weekly show-and-tell meetings, a clear task board for weekly goals, and open communication.
- Resources needed: Gemini CLI (AI agent), teachers, ChatGPT, and Gemini for external support.
- Timeline: Ongoing effort throughout the 6 weeks, with self-imposed milestones.

#### #3 Priority: Avoid Scope Creep & Rushed Features

- Rationale: To demonstrate competence and deliver a high-quality, functional product that meets the project constraints for the exam.
- Next steps: Collaborative team decisions on new features, with the teacher and AI as feasibility consultants.
- Resources needed: Ongoing brainstorming and research to evaluate feasibility, with the flexibility to cut or change features.
- Timeline: A critical decision point at 3-4 weeks to cut any features that are not progressing.
